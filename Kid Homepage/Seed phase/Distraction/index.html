Distraction


Distraction, when used skillfully, is a valuable tool for artists and sometimes the only way forward.

In meditation, the mind can be overtaken by worries or random thoughts. Many meditation schools teach mantras—automatic, repeated phrases—to prevent this. Mantras act as distractions that keep the conscious mind busy, freeing the unconscious to work. Worry beads, rosaries, and malas serve the same function.

In the creative process, stepping away from a project can create space for solutions to emerge. Holding a problem lightly in the back of our consciousness while engaging in simple, repetitive tasks—like driving, walking, swimming, or showering—can help. Physical movement may also spur ideas.

Some musicians, for example, find melodies more easily while driving than when consciously trying to compose. These distractions keep part of the mind occupied, allowing the rest to remain open to new ideas. This process of “nonthinking thought” may engage a different part of the brain, one that perceives more angles than a direct approach.

Distraction is not procrastination. While procrastination undermines creativity, distraction is a strategy that serves the work.
